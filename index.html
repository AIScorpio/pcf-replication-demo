Background

The original research

“Predictable Compression Failures: Order Sensitivity and Information Budgeting for Evidence-Grounded Binary Adjudication” (Chlon, Karim, Chlon & Awada, v3, June 2026) argues that LLM hallucination is not random noise but a predictable compression failure: when the evidence in a prompt delivers too little information relative to what a trustworthy answer requires, the model fills the gap with plausible fabrication. The paper builds an information budget (Δ̄, in nats) measuring how much the evidence actually moved the model toward the correct answer, and makes three empirical claims:

  • Exp 1 — models are sensitive to the order of evidence, this sensitivity grows logarithmically with evidence count, and averaging over shuffled orders provably improves calibration (Jensen gap > 0);
  • Exp 2 — the budget is causally linked to hallucination: randomly adding relevant evidence lowers the budget and lowers hallucination along a straight line (−12.7 pp per nat);
  • Exp 3 — a threshold on the budget-to-trust ratio (ISR ≥ 1) can gate answers on a fine-tuned model, reportedly achieving 75.9% coverage with ≤0.7% hallucination.

The paper itself frames the three experiments as an escalating ladder — its Synthesis section (§5) states verbatim: “Experiment 1 tests QMV through O(log n) dispersion and positive Jensen gaps; Experiment 2 supports EDFL operationally by showing a randomized support-dose effect on the information-budget estimate and hallucination; Experiment 3 audits the ISR=1 answer/abstain operating point.” In plain terms: validate the theory → establish the causal mechanism → deploy it as a decision rule. The sections below follow that ladder.

Why this replication

Before this mechanism informs applied-AI work on evidence-grounded workflows (document adjudication, case triage, retrieval-augmented Q&A), we needed to know: does it hold when someone else builds everything from the paper? The project replicated at maximum fidelity — three per-experiment audits (64 checkpoints) verified every traceable method choice against the paper’s verbatim text; anything the paper does not specify was quarantined and documented rather than silently chosen. Data was rebuilt from the public benchmarks per Appendix H, and runs used consumer hardware with 4-bit/bf16 quantized models as the documented platform substitution.

Findings vs the paper and its own code

What we learned by checking the paper against the authors’ repositories

The replication drew on the authors’ public code — factuality-slice (data + fine-tuning pipeline), hallbayes (the EDFL gating library) — and found the paper and its code are not the same specification:

  • The Exp 3 fine-tuning recipe is only in the code. The paper omits it; factuality-slice’s sft_gemma2.yaml (base Gemma-2-9B, bf16, LoRA r32/α64, 7 modules, 1 epoch) is the sole source, and our Exp 3 model was trained with exactly that pipeline.
  • The paper’s gate and the code’s gate differ. The text specifies ISR ≥ 1 (supervised budget-to-trust ratio); that construct does not appear in hallbayes, which instead gates on a masked-prior test (“does deleting the evidence collapse the model’s confidence?”). We ran both: the text’s gate answers nearly everything and misses insufficient-evidence traps (49/52 answered); the code’s gate rejects traps almost perfectly (2/52) but over-abstains (~20% coverage). They fail complementarily — neither reaches the paper’s operating point on our model.
  • Two errata filed against source citations during the project (both fixed in our records): a fabricated “Table 3: m=12” reading (the caption says m=16 uniformly), and a misquoted Exp 3 coverage CI ([70.1, 81.0] → the paper’s actual [72.1, 79.4], which is exactly the Wilson interval).
  • Reproducibility boundary, stated precisely: the paper’s phenomena reproduce from public information; its exact numbers do not, and each gap traces to an unpublished quantity — the Exp 3 target h*, the adjudication prompt wording, and the exact 3,059-item curation.
Transfer assessment · applied-AI memo

What this research transfers to evidence-grounded banking workflows — and what it does not

Scope discipline: every claim below is bounded by what our own frozen runs demonstrated — nothing is extrapolated from the paper’s unreproduced headline numbers.

Transfers (validated by our runs)

  • Evidence dose is a causal lever on hallucination. Adding relevant evidence monotonically cuts wrong answers (91%→26% across dose 0→3, CI excl. 0, both models). For KYC adjudication, AML case triage, or policy Q&A: retrieval recall is a first-class risk control, not a nice-to-have. Measure the dose-response curve per workflow.
  • The information budget is a strong post-hoc risk stratifier. In our audit, items where evidence pushed the model within 0.5 nats of the answer made 0/376 errors; above 1 nat, 100%. Pilot budget-style scoring in offline model-risk review before any online gating.
  • Order sensitivity is real; mixtures help. Prompt-order dispersion grows with evidence count; averaging 3–6 shuffled orders provably improves calibration (Jensen gap > 0 at every n). Use ordering ensembles for high-stakes single decisions.

Does not transfer as-is (validated failure modes)

  • The ISR ≥ 1 gate is not turnkey. Its published operating point needs an unpublished threshold and a sharply calibrated model; on our faithful fine-tune it answered 96.4% and leaked 18.1% hallucinations — and answered 49/52 insufficient-evidence traps. Do not adopt it as a compliance control without in-house calibration measurement.
  • The supervised budget needs gold labels — fine for audits, unusable as a live gate. The deployable variant (masked-prior) killed traps (2/52) but over-abstained (~20% coverage). A live gate needs both signals plus per-model threshold fitting on labeled dev data.
  • Calibration is the binding constraint, not the formula. Our model matched the paper’s accuracy (81.9% vs 80.5%) yet no gate could reach the paper’s point, because correct and wrong answers carried nearly identical confidence (0.720 vs 0.700). Measure confidence separation before promising abstention SLAs.

Implementation roadmap — how to apply this in a banking (or any evidence-grounded) setting

2 · Deploy the cheap wins (a) Set retrieval-recall targets from your dose curve and monitor per-query “evidence dose” in production as a live risk indicator; (b) for high-stakes single decisions, ensemble 3–6 evidence orderings (provably ≥ any single order by the Jensen inequality); (c) in model-risk review / QA sampling, stratify past decisions by Δ̄ and concentrate human review on the high-budget tail. Fit BOTH thresholds on your own labeled dev set. Acceptance test = trap-set rejection rate + your risk–coverage curve against the business SLA; document the (model, data, threshold) triple. Exp 3 h* sweep + masked-prior alternative-gate analysis If calibration failed in Phase 1(c) (as ours did): do not ship a gate — route by dose/budget signals to human review instead. Never adopt a published operating point: it is a property of the triple, not of the formula.

Process insights from the replication itself (governance-grade, domain-agnostic)

  • Freeze the spec before the run. Pre-registered criteria are what let us call a partially-failed replication honestly instead of moving the goalposts — the same discipline a model-risk framework expects of validation plans.
  • Every reported number must trace to a frozen artifact. Our mechanical number-tracing (report → results-file field) is directly transplantable to model documentation and audit lineage.
  • Check train/evaluation parity at the token level. A 10-second tokenizer check (does the evaluator read the exact token the model was trained to emit?) caught the one bug that would have silently invalidated an entire experiment. Institutionalize it in any fine-tune + eval pipeline.
  • Re-verify claims against primary sources. Two citation errata surfaced by simply re-reading the original paper verbatim — the same habit applies to vendor benchmark claims.
Overall conclusion

Verdict

The central thesis survives independent replication: hallucination behaves like a predictable compression failure, measurable and causally steerable through an information budget. All three experiments reproduce in direction with confidence intervals excluding zero. What does not survive is any exact headline number — most importantly the 75.9%-coverage / ≤0.7%-hallucination gate, which no threshold could reach on our faithfully fine-tuned model because its confidence barely separates right from wrong answers (0.720 vs 0.700). Practical takeaways for evidence-grounded systems: treat retrieval quality as a causal hallucination control; use budget-style scores for after-the-fact risk stratification; do not adopt any abstention gate’s published operating point without measuring your own model’s calibration first.

Appendix · replication record

How the replication was carried out — the verified final path

Everything below is the configuration that produced the frozen results above. Exploratory and superseded runs are excluded by design; they are retained in the project’s internal audit trail, not here.

A. Environment

  • Single Apple-silicon workstation (M3 Max, 64 GB unified memory); no cloud GPUs.
  • Inference/fine-tuning runtime: mlx / mlx_lm (Python 3.12 venv, pinned).
  • Documented platform substitutions: paper’s 4-bit NF4 → mlx 4-bit (group-size quantization); paper’s HF bf16 → mlx bf16 with cross-entropy computed in fp32 (mirrors HF autocast semantics).
  • Long runs executed as detached daemons with kanban progress cards and completion sentinels; every result frozen to JSON before any number was reported.

B. Data rebuild (paper Appendix H)

  • Public benchmarks: FEVER, HotpotQA, NQ-Open, PopQA + insufficient-evidence controls derived from FEVER “NOT ENOUGH INFO” claims.
  • Evidence chunks capped at 48 tokens; hard negatives mined by BM25 (≤120 per item over a ~200k-sentence corpus); per-item chunk counts spanning n ∈ [3, 60].
  • Exp 1 cohort: H.2-proportional draw rescaled to the paper’s total — the usable pool landed on exactly 3,059 items.
  • Exp 3 audit set: fixed 528 held-out items (~10% controls, BM25-padded), proven disjoint from the fine-tune’s training data (overlap = 0, checked on both question and rendered-prompt axes).

C. Fine-tune for Exp 3 (the reference recipe, verbatim)

  • Pipeline: the authors’ own factuality-slice (JSON-verdict SFT); paper omits the recipe, the repo is the sole source.
  • Base google/gemma-2-9b (not -it), bf16; LoRA r=32, α=64, dropout 0.05, 7 modules (attention + MLP) on all layers; 1 epoch, lr 1.5e-5, weight decay 0.1, cosine schedule with 3% warmup, effective batch 64, seed 42, max sequence 2048, prompt-masked loss.
  • Trained on the four non-control benchmarks; adapters fused back to bf16; training and inference at the same precision.
